To document a series of interactions I had with a specific variation of objects within my day, I decided to document doors. For this however, I will be discussing their usage and implementation within the public/private environments for which they were intended for.

 

Door knobs (Image 1: Home property bedroom door) can typically be seen in homes (especially old) and other private properties. Unlike public properties, general accessibility is not required when compared to its lever counterpart.

 

Lever Handles (Image 2: QCA campus study room door) provide more accessibility when compared of contemporary door knobs. Can be easily be utilized with appendages (elbow, wrist, foot etc.) besides the average hand. Available to be general convenient and both public and private usage.

 

Push/Pull (often denoted by their push and pull labels on either side of door) (Image 4: QCA campus Fire Exit door) and Button activated doors (Image 3: Queensland Rail train doors) are some of the many popular doors in spaces requiring accessibility. In some aspects, it has been successful in conforming to the needs of the disabled, however, not all are capable of performing the functions required to utilize these types of doors.

 

Sensor doors (Image 4: Carindale Brisbane Public Library) are normally seen in a vast amount of public environments. From shopping centres to libraries. Activation of the door requires only one simple action: a gesture or approach. Practically, this is essentially presents the most accessibility, as it  requires the least physical interaction. Especially in times of Covid-19, the lack of interaction in design now is highly valued.
