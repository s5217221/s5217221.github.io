What is Ontological Design?
The applications of ontological design influence how we live our daily lives, intuitively influencing our decisions in our days, interactions with products and media, communications typically between one another (Often complementing our way of life and tends to be taken for granted). Effectively what we design around us, in Layman’s’ terms, designs us in return.

Design concept Pillar #1: Cognition is not based on the manipulation of knowledge about the objective world.

Ukrainian artist, Oleg Shuplyak, bases his art upon this very notion. As an example, here viewers exposed to this work are provided with namely 2 interpretations: (Physically) A man and a woman resting by a tranquil lakeside, (Objectively) or an intricate design employing the use foreign elements to construct an abstract portrait. The key difference of how these can be seen is how an individually physically or objectively perceives the work.

Design Concept Pillar #2: Observers are not separate from the world he/she observes.
How can an environment include the observer? This is how interpreted the given design pillar, which pointed me towards a very interesting example of something that a majority of current society has at least encountered once in their life. The modern shopping experience. This can commonly be seen in local Australian supermarkets such as Coles and Woolworths, where they aggressively design their shopping aisles to target customers via convenient shelving height and space for name-brand products, marketing deals and discounts for specific brands with large signage and posters and other various advertising methods. The list goes on, but the use of these hard-hitting marketing tactics are an excellent instance of how certain environments are specifically designed for the interaction of its observers.

Design Concept Pillar #3: The world is created through the use of language.

Universal symbolism has become commonplace in the 21st century, and has become a social-like standard among a vast majority of countries throughout the world. With its general acceptance after a given symbol/icon’s inception, many differing companies and organisations will tend to adopt these designs, implementing them into their own productions. An excellent example of this was the origin of the “Power symbol” (often denoted by an “|” passing through “O”, referring to the computational use of 1 and 0 in Binary code), after its implementation in 1973, it has since found its way onto many User Interface designs as well as product designs and could very much be considered commonplace in our average everyday lives.

Can Designers Truly be “History Makers”?

The short answer is yes! To emphasise upon what Escobar explains, new problems arise every single day all of which require a solution. Designers create these solutions, and with these solutions become adaptions and adopted into potential future works. With this, we as humans progress in innovation, and as new designs and concepts are implemented into real world, laying the very foundations for future generations of creators to revolutionise and modernise past, present and future designs.
